# ASCCP Practice Advisory: Self Collection for Cervical Cancer Screening

> [!info] Source
> ASCCP Practice Advisory · Updated October 2025
> <https://asccp.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/ASCCP-Practice-Advisory-Self-Collection-for-Cervical-Cancer-Screening-final-updated-10-2-25.pdf>

Related notes: [[self-collected-vaginal-specimens-for-hpv-testing]] · [[hpv-human-papillomavirus]] · [[hpv-for-adults-prevention-testing-and-cancer]]

> [!abstract]
> The majority of cervical cancers in the United States occur among individuals who are unscreened or under-screened. Self-collected vaginal specimens for primary HPV screening are an opportunity to expand access. Clinician-collected samples are preferred; self-collected samples are acceptable to improve access and ease of screening.

## Background

Primary HPV screening is one of the available options for cervical cancer screening in the United States. It is the preferred method endorsed by the draft 2024 USPSTF and ACS (2020) for women ages 30–65. Other options include cytology with HPV cotesting every 5 years (acceptable), or, if HPV testing is not available, cytology alone every 3 years (acceptable).

Use of self-collected vaginal specimens for primary HPV screening is an opportunity to expand access to cervical cancer screening for unscreened and underscreened individuals.

Barriers that may be overcome by self-collection include those related to:

- **Health system** (e.g., lack of available clinicians, difficulty accessing gynecologic care)
- **Clinician** (e.g., clinician does not perform pelvic exams, lack of time in visit)
- **Patient** (e.g., limited mobility, vaginismus, history of sexual trauma, gender dysphoria, not comfortable with clinician, prefers self-collection)

## Clinician-collected vs. self-collected specimens

Clinician-collected samples are preferred; self-collected samples are acceptable to improve access and ease of screening.

Clinician-collected samples are taken from the cervix using a broom or spatula and cytobrush. This allows additional reflex testing (cervical cytology or dual stain testing) from the same specimen. Because the patient self-collected specimen is **vaginal** and the cervix is not sampled directly, the patient will need a speculum exam by a clinician if the self-collected specimen is HPV positive. Similarly, if a clinician collects a vaginal sample, it should be processed like a self-collected specimen.

## Eligibility

It is important that individuals undergoing HPV self-collection be **asymptomatic and in a routine screening situation** (i.e., not for surveillance testing or follow-up after treatment of precancer). Patients should not be on menses at the time of collection, nor have used any vaginal products for 48 hours prior to testing.

**Candidates who are NOT eligible for self-collection HPV:**

- Those whose screening recommendations are based on cytology (HIV+, DES exposed, or ages 21–24)
- Individuals who are symptomatic (e.g., abnormal bleeding)
- Individuals in surveillance following abnormal screening test results, colposcopy, or treatment

There is limited data to guide recommendations in these cases, and clinician-collected cervical specimens are preferred.

## Test performance

Overall performance of patient self-collected specimens is similar to that of clinician-collected samples **when using target amplification-based (PCR) assays**. Studies show that testing from self-collected specimens using **mRNA or signal amplification tests is less sensitive** compared with clinician-collected specimens.

## FDA-approved tests for primary HPV screening

There are currently three FDA-approved tests for primary HPV screening. Each test must use its approved self-collection device and lab platform. ASCCP does not endorse any one device or platform. (Options current as of September 2025.)

- **BD Onclarity™ HPV assay (Becton Dickinson):** Documents presence/absence of 14 high-risk HPV subtypes with extended genotyping channels: HPV16, HPV18, HPV45, HPV33/58, HPV31, HPV52, HPV35/39/68, HPV51, HPV56/59/66. Requires the Copan 522C.80 dry FLOQSwab® for self-collection.
- **cobas® (Roche):** Reports pooled detection for 12 high-risk subtypes with individual results for HPV 16 and 18. Self-collection can use either the Copan 522C.80 FLOQSwab or the Evalyn brush (Rovers Medical Devices).
- **Abbott Alinity m HR HPV assay:** Reports if high-risk HPV was detected and the type. Extended genotyping groups: HPV 16, HPV 18, HPV 45, Other HR HPV A (31/33/52/58), and Other HR HPV B (35/39/51/56/59/66/68). FDA-approved for clinician-collected cervical and self-collected vaginal specimens. Self-sampling via simpli-COLLECT™ HPV collection kit or Evalyn brush.

**The Teal Wand®** is approved for at-home self-collection for average-risk individuals aged 25–65. The patient completes a form on the company's website, is connected virtually with a provider who orders the kit and reviews results; the kit is mailed home, the patient collects with the Teal Wand® dry swab, and sends it to the lab.

### Where self-collection can be done
Abbott Alinity, BD Onclarity, and Roche cobas tests are approved for self-collection in "a healthcare setting." A healthcare setting is not strictly defined but should be a location where specimens can be processed by trained personnel and transported to a testing laboratory under controlled conditions — e.g., a hospital, emergency department, urgent care center, ambulatory clinic, physician office, mobile clinic, pharmacy, lab collection site, or a medical director-staffed school or community health clinic.

## Managing positive results

Effective management of HPV-positive self-collected test results is critical to successful implementation, especially in settings aiming to reduce disparities. This process depends on:

- Collecting reliable patient contact information at the time of self-collection for timely follow-up
- Clear, standardized tracking systems to monitor results and deliver appropriate follow-up instructions tailored to individual risk profiles
- Coordinated mechanisms — patient navigation, appointment scheduling assistance, and culturally sensitive communication — to support patients through next steps (dual stain testing, cytology, or colposcopy)

Without comprehensive systems to manage positive results and guide patients through follow-up, the effectiveness of self-collection programs may be significantly compromised, potentially delaying diagnosis and treatment.
