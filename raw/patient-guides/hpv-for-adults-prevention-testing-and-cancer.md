# HPV for Adults: Prevention, Testing, and Cancer

> [!info] Source
> American Cancer Society booklet (No. 7978.00, Rev. 11/25)
> <https://www.cancer.org/content/dam/CRC/PDF/Public/7978.00.pdf>

Related notes: [[hpv-human-papillomavirus]] · [[human-papillomavirus-and-cancer]] · [[human-papillomavirus-infection-and-vaccination]]

Human papillomavirus (HPV) has been shown to cause certain kinds of cancer. You can help lower your risk of getting an HPV-related cancer by:

- Getting vaccinated against HPV as a child or young adult (before age 27)
- Taking steps to help avoid getting the HPV virus
- Getting regular screenings to find any precancerous changes early so they can be treated before they become cancer

## What is HPV?

HPV is short for **human papillomavirus**. HPVs are a group of more than 200 related viruses. Each HPV virus has a number to identify it as an HPV type.

There are 2 main types of HPV: those that affect the skin and those that affect mucous (mucosal) membranes.

### Cutaneous (skin) HPV types
Many types of HPV invade and live in cells on the skin. They can cause warts to grow on top of the skin in areas such as hands, feet, arms, and legs. These HPV types have not been shown to cause cancer.

### Mucosal HPV types
Mucosal membranes are the moist layers that line organs and parts of the body that open to the outside. Mucosal HPV often affects the vagina, anus, cervix, vulva, or the foreskin and urethra of the penis. It can also affect the inside of the nose, mouth, and throat. Mucosal HPV types are further broken down into low- or high-risk:

**Low-risk HPV types** — These types of HPV most often cause warts on or around the genitals or anus. Warts may also grow in areas that are harder to see such as the cervix and vagina. These HPV types rarely cause cancer, so they are called low-risk. Examples of low-risk HPV types include HPV 6 and 11.

**High-risk HPV types** — Other types of HPV are called high-risk because they can cause cancer. High-risk HPV types can sometimes avoid the body's immune system. If the infection remains, it can damage normal cells. These cells can then become cancer. Common high-risk HPV types include HPV 16, 18, 31, 33, and 42.

## How do people get HPV?

Infection with HPV is common. It can pass from one person to another by intimate skin-to-skin contact, such as during sexual activity. This includes vaginal, anal, and oral sex. The virus can also be spread by genital contact without sex, although this is not common.

HPV can spread to other people even when an infected person has no signs or symptoms. In fact, a person can have HPV for years without it causing any symptoms or problems. In most cases your body's immune system gets rid of HPV on its own, often within 1–2 years following infection.

Most people who have ever had sex get at least one type of HPV at some point in their lives. Anyone who has had sex can get HPV, even if it was only with one person. But infections are more likely in people who have had many sex partners.

> [!warning] You cannot get HPV
> from toilet seats, hugging or holding hands, swimming in pools or hot tubs, sharing food or utensils, or being unclean.

You **can** have HPV even if it's been years since you were sexually active or if you do not have signs or symptoms.

## Cancers caused by HPV

### Cervical cancer
Cervical cancer (cancer of the cervix) is the most common cancer caused by HPV. In fact, nearly all cervical cancers are caused by HPV.

HPV vaccination can help protect against cervical cancer. Cervical cancer can also be found early and sometimes prevented with routine screening tests. Screening tests include HPV tests and Pap tests. HPV tests look for the infection itself. The Pap test looks for changes in cervical cells caused by HPV infection.

> [!tip]
> Cervical cancer is preventable with vaccines and regular screening tests. More than half of the people in the United States who get cervical cancer have never had — or have rarely had — screening tests.

### Vulvar cancer
HPV can also cause cancer of the vulva, which is the outer part of the female genital organs. This is much less common than cervical cancer. There's no standard screening test for this cancer. Having regular pelvic exams can help find changes or problems early.

### Vaginal cancer
Most vaginal cancers contain HPV, including most vaginal precancers. Precancers may be present for years before turning into cancer. They can sometimes be found with the same test that's used to screen for cervical cancer.

### Penile cancer
HPV can cause cancer of the penis. It's more common in men with HIV and men who have sex with other men. There's no standard screening test to find early signs of penile cancer. Because almost all penile cancers start under the foreskin of the penis, many can be found early when they are small.

### Anal cancer
HPV can cause cancer of the anus. It's more common in people with HIV and in men who have sex with other men. Screening tests for anal cancer are not recommended for all people at this time. Some experts recommend anal cytology testing for people at higher risk: men who have sex with other men, people who have had cervical or vulvar cancer, anyone who is HIV-positive, and anyone who has had an organ transplant. The anal cytology testing is called an anal Pap test.

### Mouth and throat cancer
HPV is found in mouth and throat cancers. Most cancers found in the back of the throat are HPV-related. These are the most common HPV-related cancers in men. There's no standard screening test to find these cancers early. Still, many can be found early during exams by a dentist, doctor, dental hygienist, or by self-checks.

## Can HPV infection be prevented?

Not all HPV infections can be prevented. But there are things you can do to help lower your chances of being infected.

### Use condoms
Condoms provide some protection against HPV, but they do not completely prevent infection. Condoms must be used correctly every time sex happens. They can't protect you fully because they don't cover every possible HPV-infected area of the body, such as the skin on the genital or anal area.

> [!note]
> A new condom should be used with each sex act. The condom should be put on BEFORE any genital, oral, or anal contact and kept on until sex is finished.

### Limit sex partners
Limiting the number of sex partners and avoiding sex with people who have had many other sex partners can help lower your risk of exposure to HPV.

### Get the HPV vaccine
The HPV vaccine can help prevent infection with the types of HPV that cause most HPV-related cancers. It can only prevent HPV infection; it doesn't treat a current infection. To work best, the vaccine should be given to boys and girls between the ages of **9 and 12**.

Children and young adults ages **13 through 26** who have not been vaccinated against HPV, or who haven't gotten all their doses, should get the vaccine as soon as possible. Vaccination of young adults will not prevent as many cancers as vaccination of children and teens. The American Cancer Society does not recommend HPV vaccines for anyone over 26 years of age.

> [!tip]
> HPV vaccination can help prevent more than 90% of HPV cancers when given at the recommended ages.

## Testing for HPV

HPV testing is only approved for cervical cancer screening at this time. Screening is done in people who have no symptoms of a disease.

HPV tests look for the high-risk types of HPV that are more likely to cause precancers or cancers of the cervix. Any abnormal (positive) results should be followed up with more testing and treatment if abnormal cells are found.

The American Cancer Society recommends that women and other individuals with a cervix at average risk of cervical cancer should **start screening at age 25 and continue until at least age 65**. Screening should be done every 3 to 5 years, depending on the test:

- A primary HPV test (HPV test alone) on a cervical sample collected by a health care provider every 5 years (the preferred option)
- A primary HPV test done on a self-collected* vaginal sample every 3 years
- A co-test (an HPV test combined with a Pap test) every 5 years
- A Pap test alone every 3 years, if HPV testing isn't available

*Self-collection should only be done using tests and collection devices approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA). These are only available through your health care provider.

> [!important]
> You should be screened even if you've received the HPV vaccine. Getting screened regularly is the most important thing, no matter which test you get.

## Treatment for HPV-related diseases

There's no treatment for the HPV virus. But there are treatments for cell changes that HPV can cause.

Genital warts can be treated with medicines or removed by a health care provider. Early cell changes (or precancers) caused by HPV can often be treated before they become cancer. And any cancer that is found early — while it's small and before it has spread — can more easily be treated.

## Things to remember about HPV

- HPV is a very common group of viruses. Most people who have had sexual contact will get HPV at some time in their lives.
- There is no treatment for HPV, but in most cases your immune system gets rid of the virus on its own. Having HPV does not mean you will get cancer. There are many types of HPV, and most don't cause cancer.
- Cell changes caused by HPV can often be treated so they don't turn into cancer.
- Certain people are at higher risk for HPV-related health problems. This includes men who have sex with other men and people with weak immune systems (including those who have HIV/AIDS).
- Most HPV infections that can cause cancer can be prevented with vaccines.
- Most cervical cancers can be prevented with regular screening and treatment of precancers.
