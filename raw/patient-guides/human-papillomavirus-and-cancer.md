# Human papillomavirus and cancer

> [!info] Source
> WHO Fact Sheet · Published 5 March 2024
> <https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/human-papilloma-virus-and-cancer>

Related notes: [[human-papillomavirus-infection-and-vaccination]] · [[hpv-human-papillomavirus]] · [[real-world-safety-of-hpv-vaccines-vaers]]

## Key facts

- HPV is a group of 200 known viruses that generally don't cause concern, though certain high-risk types can lead to genital warts or cancer.
- The body controls infection in approximately 90% of cases. Persistent infection with high-risk HPV types causes cervical cancer and is associated with vulvar, vaginal, throat, penile, and anal cancers.
- In 2019, HPV caused roughly 620,000 cancer cases in women and 70,000 in men.
- Prophylactic vaccination against HPV can prevent these cancers. In addition, HPV screening and treatment of pre-cancer lesions is an effective way to prevent cervical cancer.

## Overview

Human papillomavirus is a commonly sexually transmitted infection affecting nearly all sexually active individuals at some point, typically asymptomatically. It impacts skin, genital areas, and throat regions.

Condoms provide partial protection but don't cover all genital skin. Most HPV infections resolve on their own without intervention. Some trigger genital warts; others cause abnormal cellular development progressing to cancer.

Vaccines prevent HPV-related cancers effectively. The vaccine contains no live virus or viral DNA, making cancer development impossible. It prevents cancer development rather than treating existing HPV infections or diseases.

Cervical cancer remains the sole HPV-caused cancer with available screening tests. Screening identifies precancerous cellular changes before malignancy develops, enabling preventive treatment. Regular screening is essential routine healthcare for individuals with cervixes, including women and transgender men retaining cervical tissue.

Cervical cancer represents the most prevalent HPV-caused cancer type. Other less common variants affect both sexes, including anal, vulvar, vaginal, oropharyngeal, and penile cancers.

## Scope of the problem

Sub-Saharan Africa demonstrates the highest cervical HPV prevalence among women at 24%, followed by Latin America and Caribbean regions (16%), Eastern Europe (14%), and Southeast Asia (14%). Male prevalence varies considerably based on sexual patterns.

Higher prevalence occurs among women with HIV, men who have sex with men, immunocompromised individuals, those with concurrent sexually transmitted infections, recipients of immunosuppressive medications, and sexually abused children.

Approximately 620,000 new cancer cases in women and 70,000 in men resulted from HPV in 2019. Cervical cancer was the fourth leading cause of cancer and cancer deaths in women in 2022, with some 660,000 new cases and around 350,000 deaths worldwide. HPV-related cancers in women consist of cervical cancers in over 90% of instances.

Highest cervical cancer incidence and mortality rates appear in low- and middle-income countries, reflecting inequities stemming from limited access to national HPV vaccination, cervical screening, treatment services, and socioeconomic factors.

## Symptoms

Most individuals experience no HPV infection symptoms. The immune system typically eliminates HPV within one to two years without lasting consequences.

Certain HPV infections produce small rough lumps (genital warts) appearing on vaginal, penile, or anal regions and rarely the throat. These may cause pain, itching, bleeding, or glandular swelling.

Non-resolving HPV infection causes cervical cellular changes leading to precancers potentially becoming cervical cancer if untreated. Cervical cancer development typically requires 15–20 years following HPV infection.

Early cervical cellular changes and precancers rarely produce symptoms. Cervical cancer symptoms include intermenstrual bleeding, post-coital bleeding, or foul-smelling vaginal discharge. These symptoms may indicate other conditions, warranting healthcare provider consultation.

## Prevention

Vaccination represents the optimal HPV infection, cervical cancer, and HPV-related cancer prevention method. Screening detects cervical precancers treatable before progression.

Girls aged 9–14 years should receive HPV vaccination before sexual activity commences.

Administration involves one or two doses. Immunocompromised individuals require two or three doses. Healthcare providers determine optimal dosing.

Condom use during intercourse significantly reduces HPV infection risk. Voluntary male circumcision decreases infection likelihood. Non-smoking status or smoking cessation reduces chances of developing persistent HPV infection.

Cervical cell HPV testing screens women for cervical cancer. Screening should occur every 5–10 years beginning at age 30. Women with HIV require screening every 3 years commencing at age 25.

Following positive HPV testing or alternative screening methods, healthcare providers examine cervical changes or precancers potentially progressing if untreated. Precancer treatment prevents cervical cancer. Since precancers rarely cause symptoms, regular cervical health screening is critical.

Additional guidance on boys and older age group vaccination appears in the WHO position paper.

## Treatment

No current treatment exists for HPV infection itself. Treatments address genital warts, cervical precancers, and cervical cancer.

Non-cancerous genital warts and precancerous cervical, vaginal, vulvar, anal, or penile lesions undergo removal or treatment via ablation (freezing or heating) or surgical intervention.

Cervical cancer remains the sole HPV-caused cancer with available screening tests.

HPV-caused cancers, particularly cervical cancer, respond better to treatment with early diagnosis. Treatment initiation should occur promptly following diagnosis.

Management pathways for invasive cancer care facilitate patient referral and navigation through diagnosis and treatment decisions. Multidisciplinary teams ensure pre-treatment diagnosis and staging (histological testing, pathology, imaging). Treatment decisions align with national guidelines, potentially including surgery, radiotherapy, and systemic therapy such as chemotherapy. Holistic psychological, spiritual, physical, and palliative care supports interventions.

As low- and middle-income countries expand cervical screening, invasive cervical cancer detection increases, particularly in previously unscreened populations. Therefore, referral and treatment strategies require implementation and expansion alongside prevention services.

## WHO response

Acknowledging cervical cancer's substantial global public health burden from HPV, the World Health Assembly adopted the **Global strategy to accelerate cervical cancer elimination as a public health problem**, establishing these targets:

- **90%** of girls fully vaccinated with HPV vaccine by age 15
- **70%** of women screened with high-performance testing by ages 35 and 45
- **90%** of women identified with cervical disease receive treatment (90% of precancer cases treated; 90% of invasive cancer cases managed)

HPV-associated precancer and cancer prevention is a key element of the WHO Global health sector strategy addressing HIV, hepatitis, and sexually transmitted infections (2022–2030), alongside WHA74.5 (2021) resolution actions on oropharyngeal cancers.

WHO's global, regional, and national collaborative efforts with UN agencies accomplish:

1. Enhanced political commitment for policy formulation and implementation support
2. Contextualized technical assistance, lessons learned, and best-practice provision
3. Evidence-based norms and standards development
4. Global health ecosystem leadership toward target achievement and care quality improvement
