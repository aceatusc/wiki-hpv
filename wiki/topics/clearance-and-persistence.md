# Clearance & Persistence

> [!warning] Not medical advice — synthesis of sources, not a substitute for clinical care. See [[overview]].

## The established picture

In **~90%** of people the immune system controls or clears HPV, usually within **1–2 years**. `[T1]`
[[human-papillomavirus-and-cancer]] `[T3]` [[human-papillomavirus-infection-and-vaccination]] When a
high-risk infection **persists** (often defined clinically as still detectable after ~2 years, e.g.
on repeat smears), it carries the cancer risk that screening is designed to catch. `[T1]`
[[human-papillomavirus-and-cancer]] See [[cervical-dysplasia-and-precancer]].

A nuance worth recording: clinicians describe HPV as controlled/"dormant" vs fully eliminated — the
ACS notes there's **no reliable test for a person's overall "HPV status,"** and results can fluctuate
over months and years. `[T3]` [[hpv-human-papillomavirus]] This is why "is it really gone?" is
genuinely hard to answer.

## Why persistence feels so distressing

This is the single biggest gap between the evidence and the lived experience: the literature frames
clearance as the common, expected outcome, while patients with year-3+ persistent results feel
abandoned to "wait and see." That gap is where unproven remedies gain traction — see
[[supplements-and-alternative-approaches]].
